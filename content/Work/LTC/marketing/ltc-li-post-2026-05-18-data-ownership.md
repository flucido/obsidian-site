# Why Your School District Probably Doesn't Control Its Own Data

**Hook:** If your district needs a vendor support ticket to export student data, you do not fully control your own data.

---

If your district needs a vendor support ticket to export student data, you do not fully control your own data.

That is the uncomfortable reality behind a lot of K-12 data ownership language.

On paper, districts own their student information.
In practice, many districts are operating like data tenants inside vendor-controlled systems.

I keep seeing the same patterns:

• SIS exports that only run when the vendor gets involved
• assessment data trapped in proprietary formats
• attendance, discipline, and program data spread across multiple systems with no shared schema
• contracts that promise ownership but stay vague about export timelines, formats, and completeness

This is not just a technical inconvenience.

It affects how quickly district leaders can answer real questions from boards, principals, counselors, and families.
If you cannot query your own data on your own timeline, decision-making slows down across the system.

The good news: fixing it does not require replacing every platform.

It usually starts with three practical moves:

1. Audit every system for export access, format, and turnaround time.
2. Tighten contract language so open formats and response windows are explicit.
3. Pull regular copies into an internal district-controlled store.

Data sovereignty starts before the warehouse.
It starts with whether you can get your own data out, cleanly and consistently.

When was the last time your district successfully exported all of its core operational data without vendor intervention?
