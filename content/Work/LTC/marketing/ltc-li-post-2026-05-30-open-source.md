# Why Open Source Matters for School Data Infrastructure

**Hook:** Open source is not just a budget decision for schools. It is a control, continuity, and resilience decision.

---

Open source is not just a budget decision for schools. It is a control, continuity, and resilience decision.

I still hear open source framed as the cheaper but riskier option.
For school data systems, I think that framing is backwards.

The real risk often sits inside closed systems that districts cannot inspect, extend, or recover from on their own.

When a proprietary vendor changes direction, gets acquired, raises prices, or sunsets a product, the district absorbs the disruption.

With open-source infrastructure, districts have more leverage:

• the code remains available
• data workflows are easier to inspect
• integrations can be adapted over time
• institutional knowledge stays closer to the district

That does not mean open source is free of cost.
It means the investment shifts toward capacity, configuration, and stewardship instead of pure licensing.

The strongest K-12 data environments I have seen share a few traits:

• open formats
• interoperable tooling
• clear ownership of data pipelines
• fewer black boxes

That is why I think open source has a long-term place in school data infrastructure.
Not because it is trendy.
Because it supports data sovereignty.
