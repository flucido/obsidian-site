### Caption (Monday — Carousel)

We analyzed 311 therapist websites in one California county.

83% were on DIY platforms like Squarespace, Wix, and TherapySites.

Only 13% had any professional design input.

This isn't a criticism of therapists. It makes total sense — when you're building a practice, a website is one more thing on a long list, and web design agencies are expensive and often don't understand your world.

But here's what we found happening: visitors who are anxious, overwhelmed, or seeking help were landing on sites optimized for e-commerce — urgency cues, dense information, aggressive CTAs — and leaving.

Not because the practice wasn't right for them. Because the digital room felt wrong.

The good news: it's fixable. And it doesn't require a massive redesign to make a meaningful difference.

If you're a therapist and your website has ever felt "off" but you couldn't articulate why — you're not wrong. And you're not alone.

### Caption (Wednesday — Single Image)

Most websites are designed for the "average user" — someone with full cognitive availability, browsing comfortably, ready to evaluate options.

That user does not exist on a therapist's website.

The person landing on your site may be:
• Anxious about seeking help for the first time
• Overwhelmed by the number of choices
• Processing difficult emotions
• Low on the kind of energy it takes to read dense copy, interpret complex navigation, or resist urgency-clicking

When we design for the neutral user, we design against the actual visitor.

When we design for the visitor who is already activated, everyone benefits — including the visitor who's just browsing.

This is the core idea behind everything we build.

### Caption (Friday — Carousel)

A straightforward take: Squarespace is a great platform for many things. But for therapy websites, it comes with defaults that work against your visitors.

The templates are designed for visual impact — large hero images, parallax scrolling, information density. These are optimized for portfolios and e-commerce, where the goal is to impress and convert quickly.

Therapy websites have a different job: to regulate, orient, and build trust before asking for anything.

When a Squarespace template is used as-is (which 39% of therapist sites are), the visitor gets:

• Multiple competing focal points above the fold
• Navigation patterns that vary from template to template
• Mobile layouts that were resized, not redesigned
• No consideration for cognitive load

The fix isn't "get off Squarespace." The fix is: have your website designed for your actual audience, not the template's default audience.

If you're on Squarespace and your site has always felt slightly off but you couldn't name why — you're probably feeling one of these four things.
