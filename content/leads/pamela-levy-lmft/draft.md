## Pitch Strategy

**Core angle:** You already did the thoughtful part. Unlike most therapists whose sites are thin and generic, hers is substantive — a detailed "how to choose a therapist" guide, genuine service descriptions, a voice that sounds like someone who actually cares about the person on the other side of the screen. But the frame around that content is working against her: a COVID-19 banner from 2020 still sits at the top of every page, the design reads like 2012, and the dense text walls make her thoughtfulness feel like homework. She doesn't need more content. She needs the content she already wrote to breathe.

**Key pain point:** COVID-era messaging still live — a small detail that broadcasts "this site is not maintained." Combined with dated design and a generic stock photo, it creates a quiet but persistent trust tax. Her content is better than her presentation, and that gap is costing her the clients who would most appreciate what she actually offers.

**Differentiation:** Affirming rather than corrective. This is not "your site is embarrassing" — it's "your site is almost there, and you've already done the hardest part." References her actual content (the "how to choose a therapist" guide) to show genuine engagement. Respectful of her existing investment.

**CTA:** 15-minute conversation — exploratory, framed as discussing how to build on what she's already built.

You've written content that's better than 90% of therapist sites I see, and the presentation makes it harder to appreciate than it should be.

Here's what I think: you already did the hard part. The content is there. The voice is there. What's missing is a design that gives it room — clean typography, breathing space, a structure that lets someone land on your site and immediately understand who you help and how. Modern. Warm. Maintainable. No COVID banner.

I help therapists take the content they've already created and give it a home that actually serves it. For your practice — couples, women, parents in San Mateo — that means a site where your guide to choosing a therapist earns trust on landing, your specialties are clear without scrolling through paragraphs, and the first thing someone sees is your face, not a 2020 public health notice.

If you're open to it, I'd love a short conversation. Not a pitch — just a discussion about what your site could look like if it matched the care you've already put into it.
